Reading List

Research and learning resources supporting the development of Whose Plan?

This reading list supports the philosophical and psychological architecture of the essay/book project Whose Plan?

The goal is not academic specialization or metaphysical certainty.

The goal is:

  • understanding the operating tendencies of the living mind more clearly,
  • grounding contemplative inquiry in embodiment,
  • understanding cognition as adaptive organismic activity,
  • and developing a more intelligent and compassionate relationship with thought, emotion, prediction, memory, and identity.

Tier 1 — Highest Immediate Value

1. The Experience Machine — Andy Clark

Estimated Reading Time: 12–20 hours

Why It Matters:
This is probably the single most aligned book for the current direction of the essay.

Core themes include:

  • predictive processing,
  • perception as modeling,
  • anticipatory cognition,
  • uncertainty reduction,
  • recursive interpretation,
  • future simulation,
  • and embodied cognition.

This maps directly onto:

  • Chapter 9,
  • planning tangents,
  • mental rehearsal,
  • interpretation becoming reality,
  • and “advisor, not authority.”

Recommended Reading Strategy:
Do not try to master every technical detail.

Read slowly, underline conceptual insights, and continuously ask:

“How does this appear in lived experience?”

The goal is architectural understanding, not technical mastery.

2. Antonio Damasio

The Strange Order of Things

Estimated Reading Time: 10–16 hours

Why It Matters:
This strongly supports the “stay in the body” principle.

  • consciousness as embodied,
  • feelings as biological regulation,
  • organismic continuity,
  • emotional signaling,
  • and body-mind integration.

Very important for:

  • anti-metaphysical grounding,
  • embodied awareness,
  • organismic intelligence,
  • and compassionate cognitive framing.

Recommended Reading Strategy:
Read philosophically rather than academically.

3. Metacognition

Estimated Reading Time: 2–6 hours

Why It Matters:
One of the clearest scientific analogs to the essay’s observer model.

  • awareness of cognition,
  • monitoring thought,
  • executive review,
  • recursive awareness,
  • and cognitive regulation.

Most importantly:

It explains observation without requiring mystical witness metaphysics.

Recommended Reading Strategy:
Read overview articles and watch lectures rather than diving into large textbooks.

Tier 2 — Strong Supporting Architecture

4. Default Mode Network Research

Estimated Reading Time: 3–8 hours

Strongly relevant to:

  • mental wandering,
  • narrative identity,
  • future simulation,
  • self-referential thought,
  • replay,
  • and rehearsal.

Particularly useful for understanding:

  • Chapter 7,
  • Chapter 9,
  • and recursive cognitive activity.

5. Jaak Panksepp

Affective Neuroscience

Estimated Reading Time: 15–30 hours

Useful for understanding emotions as:

  • adaptive systems,
  • organismic intelligence,
  • and biological regulation.

Strong support for:

“The mind is an ally.”

Tier 3 — Valuable Deepening

6. The Embodied Mind — Francisco Varela

Estimated Reading Time: 20–35 hours

Deeply aligned philosophically with:

  • embodiment,
  • systems theory,
  • recursive awareness,
  • and organismic intelligence.

Recommended Reading Strategy:
Read slowly and revisit difficult sections.

Tier 4 — Practical Supplemental Material

7. Thinking, Fast and Slow — Daniel Kahneman

Estimated Reading Time: 15–25 hours

Useful for:

  • heuristics,
  • automatic cognition,
  • cognitive shortcuts,
  • and narrative certainty.

8. Feeling Good — David D. Burns

Estimated Reading Time: 8–15 hours

Useful for:

  • cognitive distortion,
  • event vs interpretation,
  • and escalation patterns.

Tier 5 — Use Carefully

9. Jiddu Krishnamurti

Estimated Reading Time: Potentially endless

Still highly valuable phenomenologically for:

  • direct observation,
  • deconstructing conditioning,
  • exposing identification,
  • and challenging authority.

However, readers often drift into:

  • anti-thought absolutism,
  • detached witness metaphysics,
  • transcendence narratives,
  • and spiritual superiority.

The essay should remain grounded in:

  • embodiment,
  • organismic cognition,
  • adaptive psychology,
  • and lived human experience.
