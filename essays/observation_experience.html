Observation, Experience, and Co-Arising

Most of us take reality for granted. We assume the world is simply here, existing on its own, and that we are just moving through it, observing what’s already there. That was my starting point too. I didn’t question it. It just felt obvious.

At some point, things I was reading and hearing started to loosen that assumption. I began to question it and write about it, trying to get a feel for what I believed. Early on, I was influenced by ideas from Buddhism and Hinduism. From Hinduism in particular, I picked up the sense that everything emerges from a single underlying source, appearing in different forms.

I started connecting that with what I thought I understood about quantum physics. I began to think in terms of an underlying field of possibilities, where observation doesn’t just reveal what’s there, but participates in what appears—where things manifest under specific causes and conditions at the moment of observation. That lined up, at least loosely, with the Buddhist stuff I had read about Dependent Origination.

At that point, a further question came up. If everything arises in response to observation, then what is the observer? Is it something separate from what is observed, or is it also something that arises under conditions? If it arises under causes and conditions, does something observe that?

That question started to shift how I saw things. Even in measurement, the observer is not outside the system. The measuring instrument participates in what is measured. What is observed does not come from a detached act of viewing, but from interaction.

A simple example: if you want to measure an electrical circuit, you use a meter. But the meter itself has electrical properties. When you connect it, it becomes part of the circuit and changes it. There is no way to measure the “pure” circuit from the outside without affecting it. The circuit you are reading already includes the meter. In effect, by measuring it, you’ve created a slightly different circuit.

Our senses are instruments. Perception is not passive reception but active engagement. There is no clear standpoint outside the system from which reality can be observed “as it is.” Everything we know comes through this kind of interaction.

If you dip your finger into a liquid to check its temperature, what follows is not two separate things simply interacting. Water penetrates the outer layer of your skin while heat flows through both the liquid and your tissue. The boundary between “finger” and “liquid” becomes less clear at the level where the measurement happens. The water, the finger, the nervous system, and the brain are inseparable. The temperature you feel does not belong to the liquid or to your finger alone. It arises from the whole.

If you stand in a room and look into a mirror, you can see an image of yourself in that room. In that moment, you are part of the system that produces what you see. The room, the light, the mirror, your eyes, and your nervous system come together to produce the image. Your memory and mind organize what is seen into the concept of “you in a room.” You are not outside of it. You are part of what makes the observation possible.

If you leave the room, the mirror remains. Light still reflects off surfaces and reaches it. But without an observer, the mirror is just bouncing photons around. There is no image being seen. There is no concept of a “room” as it appears in experience. The structure may still exist in some physical sense, but the room—as it is known—does not arise.

Now suppose you place a camera in the room and view the mirror remotely. It may seem like you are no longer part of the system, but the system has expanded to include the camera. What once was you in the room with the mirror, your eyes, and your brain is now the room, the mirror, the camera, your eyes, and your brain—you still have to look at the camera feed.

Your brain is still part of the system, so the room is still perceived according to the nature of your sense organs and your nervous system. The image depends on how your system processes the incoming signals.

If you had the sense organs and nervous system of a fly, what appears as a “room” would be entirely different, and it would not organize into anything like a “room,” because flies cannot think the way we do. Likewise, to a color-blind individual, the same space would present differently.

What is experienced is shaped by the structure of the system through which it appears. That structure is what we call the observer.

So What?

It is fair at this point to ask, “So what?” The point is, contrary to our usual way of thinking, we are not a thing moving through a world. We are inseparable from it. We are not moving through reality. The whole thing is a movement, and we are part of it.

It’s like a wave on the surface of the ocean. The wave might appear to be something moving across the water, but it isn’t separate from it. The wave is the ocean, waving.

There is a commonly quoted question: “If a tree falls in a forest and no one is there, does it make a sound?”

If I am not present, there is no tree or forest for me. This is not a claim about what ultimately exists. It is a statement about experience. “Tree” and “forest” are not encountered outside perception, memory, or inference. Outside experience, those categories do not apply. They are products of a categorical mind.

The same applies to the observer. The sense of “me” is not a solid, fixed point from which experience is viewed. It arises along with what is perceived. The tree, the forest, and the sense of self emerge together under specific conditions. None precede the others. Subject and object are not separate things that later interact; they are aspects of a single event.

From this perspective, there is no single, final version of a tree, a forest, or a self. There are endlessly varying configurations of conditions. No configuration is privileged. What appears is always contingent, always in flux.

None of this requires special insight or altered states. Ordinary experience is enough. What matters is not discovering a hidden reality, but understanding how experience unfolds and how suffering arises within it.

This view avoids two common extremes. It does not posit a fundamental observer, a universal consciousness, or an underlying substance. It also does not collapse into nihilism. Experience occurs. Appearances arise conditionally. That is enough.

Meaning and responsibility do not depend on metaphysical certainty. Suffering is real as experience. So is relief. Compassion and action arise within the same conditions as everything else.

What remains is simple: co-arising, impermanence, and the practical task of reducing suffering within what appears.
