Programming in the Age of AI

A Note to New Computer Science Graduates

WebWork Essays

Students graduating from computer science programs today are entering the profession at a moment that feels uncertain. Artificial intelligence tools can now generate code, scaffold applications, and translate natural language into working software. It is natural for new graduates to ask a difficult question: if AI can write code, what happens to programmers?

The concern is understandable. But history suggests that this moment is not the end of programming. It is the next stage in a long pattern that has shaped computing since its beginning. To see where the profession may be heading, it helps to look at how programming has evolved over time.

The Ladder of Abstraction

Programming has always progressed through increasing levels of abstraction. Each major development in computing moved programmers further away from the raw hardware and closer to the problem they were trying to solve. At each stage, tools automated part of the work programmers once performed manually. Yet programmers did not disappear. Their role moved upward.

Era Human Role
Machine Code Machine operator
Assembly Instruction author
High-level languages Algorithm designer
Framework era System integrator
Platform era System architect
AI-assisted era Problem specifier

In the earliest days of computing, programmers worked directly with machine instructions. Programs consisted of numeric codes representing operations executed by the CPU. Assembly language improved this by introducing symbolic instructions, but developers were still thinking in terms of registers, addresses, and memory locations.

High-level languages changed the game by allowing programmers to express algorithms rather than individual machine operations. Frameworks and libraries later allowed developers to assemble larger systems from reusable parts. Cloud platforms abstracted even more by moving infrastructure management into shared services. The pattern remained consistent: complexity was pushed downward into tools, while human attention moved upward toward design.

What AI Actually Changes

AI-assisted development tools are another step in this progression. They are highly effective at producing local solutions. They can generate functions, boilerplate, tests, and common application structures with impressive speed. Tasks that once required hours of typing can now take minutes.

But writing code is only one part of building software. Real systems involve deeper questions: What architecture should be used? How should components communicate? How will the system scale under load? What security risks must be handled? How will the system behave when parts fail?

AI tools can generate fragments of code, but they do not automatically understand the full purpose, constraints, and tradeoffs of a system. They can assist with implementation, but responsibility for coherence still belongs to engineers. That is why this moment should be understood not as the removal of programmers, but as a change in what programmers spend their time doing.

The Emerging Role: Problem Specifier

As AI tools improve, the role of the developer gradually shifts from code author to problem specifier. Instead of writing every detail manually, engineers increasingly define architecture, specify behavior and constraints, guide AI tools toward useful outputs, evaluate generated solutions, and integrate components into working systems.

In this model, AI behaves less like a replacement engineer and more like a highly capable collaborator. But collaboration still requires direction. A person who understands computer science concepts can guide these tools effectively. A person who does not understand the system cannot. The skill that matters most is no longer just the ability to write code quickly. It is the ability to understand problems clearly and shape solutions intelligently.

Automation does not remove the need for engineers. It raises the level at which engineers work.

What Your CS Degree Actually Trains You For

Computer science education is sometimes mistaken for training in programming languages. In reality, it teaches something deeper. A strong CS curriculum introduces the fundamental structures of computation: algorithms, data structures, operating systems, networking, concurrency, distributed systems, and system architecture. These concepts are not tied to any particular language or tool. They are ways of thinking about how complex computational systems behave.

AI tools may generate code that compiles and runs, but they do not automatically produce efficient algorithms, scalable architectures, secure systems, or reliable infrastructure. Those outcomes require engineering judgment. And engineering judgment comes from understanding the underlying principles of computation.

When code generation becomes easier, the most valuable skill becomes knowing what code should exist in the first place. That is exactly the skill computer science programs are meant to develop. Students trained in architecture, systems, and problem decomposition are not being pushed out of the field. They are being prepared for the layer of the field that matters most when raw implementation becomes cheaper.

A Pattern Repeating Itself

Worries about automation replacing programmers have appeared many times before. Developers once worried that compilers would eliminate assembly programmers, that high-level languages would eliminate systems programmers, that frameworks would eliminate application developers, and that cloud platforms would eliminate infrastructure engineers. Each of these technologies did automate part of the programming process. And each of them created new layers of work.

Instead of disappearing, programmers moved upward into roles requiring broader understanding. The same pattern appears again today. AI tools automate certain coding tasks, but they increase the importance of system design, architecture, integration, evaluation, and judgment. In other words, they move developers further up the abstraction ladder.

Where the Field May Be Heading

If current trends continue, the next stage of programming may involve coordinating multiple intelligent tools. Instead of writing large programs directly, engineers may guide systems that generate, test, and deploy software automatically.

In this environment, the human engineer becomes the architect and orchestrator of the system rather than the person responsible for every line of implementation code. This does not eliminate programming. It changes what programming means.

For Students Entering the Field

Graduates entering the profession today will likely become the first generation of engineers who regularly collaborate with AI during software development. The tools they use may look different from those used by earlier programmers. But the skills that matter most remain the same: understanding complex systems, reasoning about algorithms, designing architectures, evaluating tradeoffs, and integrating components into reliable systems.

These are precisely the abilities that computer science education is meant to develop. The tools will change. Languages will change. Workflows will change. But the need for people who understand computation deeply will not disappear.

The Core Pattern

If the history of programming teaches one lesson, it may be this: automation does not eliminate engineers. It changes the level at which engineers work. Each generation of tools removes some mechanical work and allows developers to focus on deeper conceptual problems. Artificial intelligence appears to be the next step in that long progression.

For new graduates, this moment should not be viewed as the end of the profession. It is the next invitation to climb higher on the ladder.
