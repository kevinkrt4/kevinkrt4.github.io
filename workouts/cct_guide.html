Training for Speed and Power

Principles and execution of Complex Contrast Training

Overview

This workout borrows some ideas from research on Post-Activation Performance Enhancement (PAPE) to improve power output. Research suggests that a heavy contraction can temporarily enhance subsequent explosive performance through increased motor unit recruitment and neural drive (Thapa et al.).

Each workout is arranged in exercise blocks, where a heavy lift primes the system and is followed by an explosive movement to express that power. Research is ongoing, but regardless of the exact mechanisms, this is a fun and challenging format that emphasizes focus, intent, and high-quality movement.

Core Principles

Speedwork is built on intent, not appearance. With lighter loads, the bar moves quickly; with heavier loads, it moves more slowly. This difference in bar travel is expected. What matters is how force is applied.

Intent means contracting your muscles as fast as possible on every rep. Even when the bar moves slowly under heavy load, the muscles should still be firing rapidly. The goal is to produce force through rapid contraction.

Rest and volume must be managed to preserve power output. This work depends on being fresh enough to produce fast, high-quality reps. When fatigue accumulates, speed drops and the training effect shifts away from power.

Stability and control apply to all movements. Each rep should begin from a stable position, maintain alignment, and direct force into the intended movement. Unintended shifting or use of momentum usually reflects poor control rather than load selection.

Movements should be performed as intended—strict when strict, dynamic when dynamic—but always under control.

In Short

  • Contract muscles as fast as possible on every rep
  • Accept that bar speed will vary with load
  • Manage rest and volume to preserve power
  • Maintain stability and control
  • Stop sets when speed declines

Train for rapid force production, not fatigue.

Load Selection

If the weight is too light, it will move quickly but require little effort, limiting its carryover to strength and power. This often shows up as movement that feels effortless, lacks resistance at the top, or becomes loose and uncontrolled.

The goal is to use a load that allows you to move quickly while still requiring intentional force and control. If it feels like you are just throwing it up there, the load is too light. When the weight is correct, reps feel both fast and forceful, with consistent control throughout the movement.

Load Targets (% of 5RM)

  • Heavy: ~85–100%
  • Explosive: ~60–75% (or bodyweight / implements)
  • Speed (optional): ~50–65%

RPE Targets

  • Heavy: RPE 8–9
  • Explosive: RPE 5–7
  • Speed: RPE 4–6

Block Structure

Each block follows the same pattern used in the CCT workout: a heavy strength movement followed by an explosive movement.

  • A1 / B1 / C1: Heavy strength movement
  • Rest: 2–4 minutes
  • A2 / B2 / C2: Explosive movement
  • Rest: 3–5 minutes before repeating the block
  • Heavy reps: 3–6
  • Explosive reps: 5–8
  • Sets: 3–4 per block

The goal is to keep the explosive movement fast and powerful. If speed drops, reduce load, reduce reps, or stop the block.

Execution Rules

  • Heavy sets should be challenging but controlled
  • Explosive sets must be fast and crisp
  • Stop if speed drops
  • Prioritize quality over fatigue

Common Mistakes

  • Going too heavy and losing speed
  • Going too light and losing force
  • Not resting long enough
  • Letting fatigue dominate the session

What It Should Feel Like

The session should feel fast, sharp, and controlled, with full recovery between efforts and a sense of increased power after heavy work.

If you feel out of breath or slowed down, the session has shifted away from speed training.

Conditioning

Conditioning and low-intensity cardio are optional at the very end of the workout.

Sled work can be used to extend force production with relatively low recovery cost. Keep efforts short (20–40 yards), controlled, and powerful.

Low-intensity cardio can follow to support recovery and general conditioning. Keep it easy and controlled.

  • If reps stayed fast and consistent, sled work can be added
  • If speed dropped or technique broke down, skip conditioning
  • If you feel sharp, add sleds
  • If fatigued or drained, end the session
